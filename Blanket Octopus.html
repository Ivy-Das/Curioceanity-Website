    • Blanket Octopus

    Blanket octopi are mostly found on the surface of tropic or sub-tropic regions of the oceans. They get their name due to the fact that their tentacles are connected to each other by thin blanket like structures or excess skin-webs. The male and juvenile female blanket octopodes cut off the poisonous Portuguese man o' war tentacles and use them for both aggressive and defensive purposes, however blanket octopodes are immune to its venom. The blanket octopus, like many other octopuses, squirts ink at potential predators to terrify them.

    Additionally, the female expands her huge membranes, which resemble a net, when she feels threatened, making herself appear much larger than she actually is. They seek for food while residing in coral reefs. Most of their prey can be found in or close to coral reefs, and their diet consists primarily of smaller fish. In the reef, they may also hide from larger fish and whales, two of their main predators. The coral reefs on which the Blanket Octopus is strongly dependent are under grave risk as a result of human-caused climate change. Coral bleaching and ocean acidification are two threats to these reefs. The Blanket Octopus is a nomadic species that can move around and find shelter elsewhere.

    They also have the ability to adapt to changing ocean temperatures, giving them two defense mechanisms that protect them from the effects of climate change. Despite the fact that this can be dangerous for the Blanket Octopus because it is their habitat, these animals have two defense mechanisms that protect them from these changes. The sexual size-dimorphism of this octopus is among the most dramatic yet observed in an animal its size or greater. Males are only 2.4 cm long, whereas females can grow to a length of 2 m.

    The weight ratio ranges from 10,000:1 to 40,000:1. The males have a huge arm inside of a hectocotylus, a round pouch modified for mating. This arm is separated during mating and maintained by the female in her mantle cavity until it is utilized for fertilization. After mating, the male typically passes away. Males compete with one another, as evidenced by the presence of several male arms in females' mantle cavities. More than 100,000 eggs are carried by the females on a sausage-shaped calcareous secretion that is kept at the base of the dorsal arms and carried by the female till hatching.
