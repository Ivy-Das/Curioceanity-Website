    • Sei Whale

    Sei whales belong to the family Balaenoptera borealis and is a type of baleen whale. It found mostly in deep offshore waters. It is 68 feet long and weighs 28 tons. Sei whales mostly eat copepods and krill. This species of whale is also threatened due to increased poaching and hunting for commercial sale before 2008. Now, due to a declaration made by the UN it is illegal to hunt sei whales. Pollock, also known as coalfish and a close relative of codfish, is referred to as "sei" in Norwegian. Both the pollock and the sei whales first came off the Norwegian coast at the same period, traveling there to eat the plentiful plankton. The particular name is the northern-sounding Latin word borealis. The epithet "finner" was frequently used to describe rorquals. In the Pacific, the whale has been referred to as the Japan finner.

    The whale's name in Japanese is iwashi kujira, or sardine whale; it was first used by early Japanese whalers to refer to Bryde's whales. Later, it was mistaken for the sei whale when modern whaling moved to Sanriku, where both species are found. After the blue whale (up to 180 tonnes, 200 tons) and the fin whale (up to 70 tonnes, 77 tons), the sei whale is the third-largest balaenopterid, however it is relatively small compared to the humpback whale. Adult males and females in the North Pacific average 13.7 meters (45 feet) and 15 meters (49 feet), respectively, and weigh 15 and 18.5 tonnes (16.5 and 20.5 tons), whereas those in the North Atlantic average 14 meters (46 feet) and 14.5 meters (48 feet), respectively, and weigh 15.5 and 17 tonnes (17 and 18.5 tons). The body of the whale is usually dark steel grey with sporadic light grey to white patterns on the ventral surface or in the front of the lower body. Comparatively speaking to other species, the whale has a very brief set of 32 to 60 pleats or grooves along its ventral surface that limit the enlargement of the buccal cavity while feeding.

    The pectoral fins are only 9% to 10% of body length, are relatively short, and are pointed at the tips. The rostrum is pointed. The rostrum tip to the pair of blowholes that are a defining feature of baleen whales are connected by a single ridge. This rorqual feeds by filtering through water with its baleen plates, opening its mouth to ingest or skim huge quantities of the water holding the food, then straining the water out through the baleen, trapping any food particles inside. Averaging roughly 900 kg of food each day, the sei whale feeds close to the ocean's surface by swimming through swarms of prey while lying on its side.

    Generally speaking, given an animal of its size, its favored foods—such as zooplankton and small fish—lie abnormally comparatively low in the food chain. Long, low-frequency sounds are produced by sei whales. Although specific cries are not well understood, observers have heard sei whale calls as well as sounds that might be "growls" or "whooshes" off the coast of the Antarctic Peninsula. Numerous calls included numerous portions that were broadcast at various frequencies. This combination sets their calls distinct from those of other whales. Most calls lasted around half a second and took place in the audible range of 240 to 625 hertz. The vocal sequences have a maximum volume of 156 dB. A vocalizing whale would sound like a jackhammer working two meters away to someone standing one meter away.

    Scientists captured sei whale cries off the coast of Maui in November 2002. All of the calls were downswept tonal calls, with the exception of two that went from an average high frequency of 39.1 Hz down to 21 Hz during a 1.3 second period. The two higher frequency downswept calls ranged from an average high frequency of 100.3 Hz to 44.6 Hz over a one second period. The sei whale typically makes an annual migration from cool, subpolar waters in the summer to temperate, subtropical areas in the winter when there is more food available. Sightings and catch records in the northwest Atlantic indicate that the whales migrate north along the shelf edge, arriving in the Georges Bank, Northeast Channel, and Browns Bank regions by mid- to late June.

    In August and September, they can be found off the south coast of Newfoundland. From mid-September to mid-November, they migrate southbound along the Nova Scotian shelf. Later in the summer, whales in the Labrador Sea as early as the first week of June can migrate further north to waters southwest of Greenland. The development of explosive harpoons and steam-powered whaling ships in the late nineteenth century brought previously unobtainable large whales within reach of commercial whalers. Initially their speed and elusiveness, and later the comparatively small yield of oil and meat partially protected them. Once stocks of more profitable right whales, blue whales, fin whales, and humpback whales became depleted, sei whales were hunted in earnest, particularly from 1950 to 1980. Before 1970, when the International Whaling Commission first established capture quotas for the North Pacific for specific species, the sei whale lacked significant international protection. There were no legal restrictions before quotas. In the North Pacific, complete protection against commercial whaling began in 1976.

    In the North Atlantic, quotas on sei whales were introduced in 1977. Stocks in the Southern Hemisphere were safeguarded in 1979. The International Whaling Commission (IWC) banned commercial whaling entirely starting in 1986 in response to mounting evidence that several whale species were in danger of going extinct. Some "pirate" whaling occurred in the eastern North Atlantic in the late 1970s. Although there is no concrete proof of illegal whaling in the North Pacific, catch records are not totally credible due to the Soviet Union's acknowledged falsification of whaling data. Sei whale major extinctions have been documented for many years, and evidence implies that endemic poisoning (red tide) reasons may have contributed to mass extinctions in the distant past. The largest mass beaching ever recorded occurred in June 2015, when 337 dead sei whales were discovered by scientists flying over southern Chile. Although the exact origin is unknown, the Blob, an extraordinary warming event in the Pacific Ocean, may be to blame for harmful algal blooms.
