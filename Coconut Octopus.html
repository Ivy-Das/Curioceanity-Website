    • Coconut Octopus

    Coconut octopus or Amphioctopus marginatus is an octopus of genus Amphioctopus. They are found in tropical waters of the western Pacific Ocean and mostly eats shrimp, crabs, and clams. They get their name due to their behaviours like walking on two tentacles and collecting coconut shells or other objects to make a home. The octopus's main body is typically 8 centimeters (3 in) long, and when the limbs are added, it measures about 15 centimeters (6 in) long.

    The octopus has a characteristic color pattern that consists of a yellow siphon and dark ramified lines that resemble veins. The arms typically have contrast white suckers on black arms. A lighter trapezoidal region can be visible directly below the eye in many color displays. The sandy bottom of bays or lagoons is where you can find the coconut octopus. With only its eyes exposed, it frequently buries itself in the sand. A. marginatus was said to move on two feet in a publication from University of California, Berkeley researchers that appeared in Science in March 2005. Only two octopus species—the other being Abdopus aculeatus—have been found to exhibit this behavior. In a place off Sulawesi, Indonesia, where the sandy bottom was covered in coconut shells, the scientists came across this behavior. The bipedal movement seems to resemble a coconut floating in midair.

    According to Australian researchers from the Melbourne Museum, the coconut octopus exploits available debris to build a defensive fortress as a means of defense and camouflage. Inhabitants of Bali and North Sulawesi in Indonesia have been seen engaging in this practice. The scientists captured footage of the octopus removing human-discarded coconut shell fragments from the ocean floor. They were then lifted up to 20 meters in the air and placed in a clam-shell-shaped configuration around the octopus's body.
